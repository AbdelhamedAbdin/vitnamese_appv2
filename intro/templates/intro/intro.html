{% trans 'Corpus Description' %}

{% blocktrans %}Chinese-character-handwriting (CCW) has been acknowledged as the most challenging aspect of Chinese literacy due to the special properties of Chinese logography. Students who learn Chinese as a second language (CSL) usually lag in and get intimidated by learning CCW (Ke, 1996). To deepen the understanding of what difficulties CSL students face and how they learn CCW within different L1 language backgrounds, and further provide pedagogical benefit that are based on first-hand authentic materials, we set to construct a learner corpus of CCW error and make it reachable as an online resource for researchers and educators.{% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans %}The learner corpus of CCW error is compiled by two sets of data. The first set was collected through a writing-to-dictation test of Chinese simplified characters by *** Vietnam university students who are majoring in Chinese. The second set was collected via a delayed copying of ** Chinese concrete words (traditional characters) by *** NCS non-Chinese-speaker (NCS) students in Hong Kong.{% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans %}The tag-set for error-annotation is conducted within a framework called the four-level composition of Chinese characters (Leung, 2009; Liang, 2019). According to this framework, character is the basic unit of Chinese handwriting. Three tiers of sub-character constituents are involved in the composition of characters (Table 1). Strokes are the smallest constituents rendered when a pen touches and leaves a paper. Radicals refer to phonetic or semantic radicals which give clues to sound or meaning of the compound, the major group of Chinese characters (over 80% (Shu & Anderson, 1997)). Logographemes are composed by strokes directly and recur consistently and independently as integral constituents in radicals or characters (Lau, 2020).{% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans %}CCW errors traditionally have been divided into two kinds: cuozi (misspelling) in which the target character is spelled incorrectly, and biezi (alternative spelling) where another character (which itself is spelled correctly) is substituted for the target character (Shen & Bear, 2000). According to our framework, biezi is annotated as an error at the whole-character level, while cuozi could be subsumed further into various sub-types according to the sub-character constituent that is attributed to the error, which are summarized in Table 2.{% endblocktrans %}
